Research Theme

Exoplanetary Science & Habitability

Detecting new worlds beyond the Solar System, characterising their orbits and atmospheres, and assessing what makes a planet habitable — including the role of the host star and the wider planetary system.

Exoplanets Astrobiology Habitability Atmospheres System Architecture

Overview

Tens of thousands of confirmed and candidate exoplanets now span an enormous diversity of sizes, compositions, and orbits. The questions are shifting from "do other worlds exist?" to "what are they like, and could any of them host life?".

QUASAR researchers contribute across this stack — from photometric and spectroscopic surveys through dynamical modelling to atmospheric characterisation and habitability assessment.

Key science questions

  • How common are habitable-zone planets, and how do their occurrence rates depend on host star properties?
  • What can transmission and emission spectra tell us about exoplanet atmospheres and climates?
  • How do planetary systems form and dynamically rearrange over time?
  • What roles do stellar activity, winds, and flares play in shaping planetary atmospheres?
  • How should "habitability" be defined and tested across diverse planetary contexts?

Approach & methods

  • Transit photometry from space- and ground-based facilities.
  • High-precision radial velocity follow-up and orbit characterisation.
  • Atmospheric spectroscopy and retrieval modelling.
  • Dynamical and population modelling of multi-planet systems.
  • Joint stellar / planetary modelling for habitability assessment.
