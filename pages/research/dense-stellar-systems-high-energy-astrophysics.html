Research Theme

Dense Stellar Systems & High-Energy Astrophysics

Modelling stars and compact objects in some of the universe's most extreme environments — globular clusters, nuclear star clusters, and merging binaries — to understand how black holes, neutron stars, and high-energy transients form, interact, and radiate.

Black Holes Gravitational Waves N-Body Sims Compact Binaries Multi-Messenger

Overview

Dense stellar systems are natural laboratories for some of the most exotic processes in astrophysics. Frequent gravitational encounters reshape stellar populations, drive the formation of compact binaries, and seed gravitational-wave sources detectable across the universe. The same systems power high-energy transients ranging from X-ray binaries to short gamma-ray bursts.

QUASAR researchers connect dynamical simulations to multi-messenger observations to understand how dense environments shape the populations of compact objects we now routinely detect.

Key science questions

  • How do binary black holes and neutron stars form, evolve, and merge?
  • What seeds intermediate-mass and supermassive black holes in early dense environments?
  • How do globular clusters and nuclear star clusters dynamically process stars and binaries?
  • What can multi-messenger detections tell us about compact-object populations and host environments?
  • How do the highest-energy electromagnetic transients connect to compact-object physics?

Approach & methods

  • Direct N-body and Monte-Carlo simulations of dense stellar systems.
  • Population synthesis of binary stars and compact objects.
  • Coordinated electromagnetic follow-up of gravitational-wave events.
  • High-energy spectroscopy and timing of compact-object systems.
  • Statistical inference on growing catalogues of gravitational-wave detections.
