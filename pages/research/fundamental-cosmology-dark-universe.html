Research Theme

Fundamental Cosmology & The Dark Universe

Mapping the geometry, expansion history, and contents of the universe to understand dark energy, dark matter, the Hubble Tension, and whether general relativity remains the right description of gravity at the largest scales.

Dark Energy Hubble Tension Testing Gravity Dark Matter Large-Scale Structure

Overview

Modern cosmology has converged on a remarkably successful but deeply puzzling model: a universe whose energy budget is dominated by components — dark matter and dark energy — that we cannot directly detect in the laboratory. Cosmic microwave background, large-scale structure, and supernova observations agree to within a few percent on the broad-brush picture, yet leave the fundamental nature of these components open.

QUASAR researchers work across surveys, simulations, and statistical inference to sharpen precision tests of this model and to look for cracks that might point beyond it.

Key science questions

  • Is dark energy a true cosmological constant, or does it evolve with time?
  • What is the origin of the persistent tension between local and early-universe measurements of the Hubble constant?
  • Can modified theories of gravity reproduce cosmic acceleration without invoking new particles or fields?
  • What does the growth of cosmic structure tell us about neutrino masses and the dark sector?
  • How do we extract robust cosmological constraints from increasingly large, systematics- limited datasets?

Approach & methods

  • Galaxy redshift surveys, baryon acoustic oscillations, and weak gravitational lensing.
  • Type Ia supernova distance measurements and standard-candle calibrations.
  • Cosmic microwave background analysis and joint likelihoods with low-redshift probes.
  • N-body and hydrodynamical simulations of cosmic structure formation.
  • Bayesian parameter estimation, model selection, and machine-learning-assisted inference.
